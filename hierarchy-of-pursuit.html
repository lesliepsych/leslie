Hierarchy of Pursuit:
Knowing Your Goals

Not all goals are created equal — and the one you're chasing right now
says everything about who you're becoming.

Leslie A. Esperida
February 12, 2026
10 min read

Most of us never stop to ask ourselves the one question that shapes nearly every decision we make: What am I actually pursuing? Not what we tell others — but the real, driving goal underneath all the effort, the hustle, and the sacrifice.

When I work with people navigating burnout, identity confusion, or a quiet sense that something is missing even when life "looks good on paper," I often find the same root cause: a misalignment between the goal they're living for and the goal that would actually fulfill them.

Over time, I've come to think of our pursuits as existing on a kind of hierarchy — a layered spectrum from the most surface-level motivations to the deepest, most enduring ones. None of these goals are inherently wrong. But understanding where you are on this spectrum — and where you want to be — can be genuinely life-changing.

The Eight Levels of Pursuit

Think of this less like a ladder of judgment and more like a map of self-awareness. The goals at the base aren't bad — they're simply the ones most shaped by external validation, social comparison, and circumstance. As you move upward, the goals become more internally anchored, more resilient, and more generative.

Hierarchy of Pursuit — from immediate gratification to enduring meaning

Let's explore each level — honestly and without judgment. You may recognize yourself in more than one.

· · ·

A Closer Look at Each Goal

1
Happiness
Pursuing comfort, pleasure, and the absence of pain

On the surface, "I just want to be happy" sounds like the most reasonable thing a person could say. But when happiness itself becomes the primary pursuit — the thing we arrange every decision around — it tends to become surprisingly elusive.

The trap: Happiness is a byproduct, not a destination. Research in positive psychology consistently shows that people who directly chase happiness report lower well-being than those who pursue meaning, connection, and growth — and find happiness as a side effect. When we optimize only for feeling good, we often make the choices that undermine our long-term flourishing.

That said, joy, rest, and pleasure are not frivolous. They are necessary. The question is whether happiness is your compass or your reward.

2
Cool
Pursuing aesthetic, image, and the admiration of observers

"Cool" is about perception management — the clothes, the tastes, the effortless air of someone who seems to have figured something out. It's adolescent in origin but disturbingly present in adult life, from Instagram aesthetics to career posturing.

The trap: Cool is fundamentally about audience. It requires witnesses. And maintaining an image is exhausting work that gradually hollows out the self underneath it. When your energy goes into performing, it stops going into becoming.

There's nothing wrong with caring about how you present yourself. But ask: are you dressing your life up, or are you building one?

3
Popularity
Pursuing approval, belonging, and widespread likability

Popularity is the pursuit of being broadly liked — to have many people in your corner, to be included, celebrated, talked about warmly. In a world of follower counts and viral moments, this goal has never had more infrastructure built around it.

The trap: The pursuit of widespread approval requires managing everyone's perception of you — which means being slightly different things to slightly different people, until you're not quite sure who you actually are. Authenticity and popularity are not enemies, but they do pull in opposite directions when popularity becomes the priority.

Human beings need genuine belonging. But belonging to yourself comes first.

4
Prestige
Pursuing status, rank, and the respect of peers and institutions

Prestige is a step up from popularity — it's not about everyone liking you, but about the right people respecting you. The prestigious career, the elite credential, the title that turns heads in a specific room. This is a deeply human drive, and there's real psychological payoff when it's achieved.

The trap: Prestige is defined by the hierarchy you've agreed to play within. If your self-worth is anchored to a ranking you didn't design, you will spend your life serving someone else's definition of success. Many people reach the top of their prestige ladder and feel, with quiet horror, that they climbed the wrong wall.

Ask yourself honestly: whose scoreboard are you playing on?

5
Money
Pursuing financial security, freedom, and material abundance

Money is placed here not because it's unimportant — it absolutely is — but because it is most powerful as a tool rather than a terminal goal. Financial security reduces anxiety, expands options, and creates the conditions for other pursuits to flourish. Wanting to build wealth is rational and valid.

The trap: Money is infinitely scalable as a goal, which means it can consume an entire life without natural stopping points. "More" always exists. When money becomes the point rather than the platform, people often discover too late that it was filling the space where meaning was supposed to live.

Money answers the question of what you can do. It doesn't answer the question of what you should do.

6
Excellence
Pursuing mastery, craft, and the relentless raising of your own standard

This is where the pursuit starts to turn genuinely inward. Excellence is the commitment to doing something exceptionally well — not for the audience's applause, not for the rank it earns, but because the work itself demands it. The craftsperson, the scholar, the athlete who pushes past what anyone else requires of them.

The gift: Excellence is largely within your control. You can pursue it in obscurity, in difficulty, in circumstances that offer no external reward. It builds a deep and durable sense of self-respect that social validation cannot provide and cannot take away.

The watch-out: Excellence without purpose can become perfectionism. Mastery for its own sake can slide into isolation or rigidity. Excellence is most alive when it is pointed at something beyond itself.

7
Impact
Pursuing meaningful contribution to others' lives and the world

Impact is excellence with a direction. It asks not just "how good am I at this?" but "who is better because I exist?" It's the teacher who shapes how a generation thinks. The researcher whose work outlives them. The parent who raises a child into a person of depth and integrity.

The gift: Impact is one of the most reliable sources of enduring meaning. Viktor Frankl, writing from the depths of a Nazi concentration camp, observed that those who survived longest were those who had a reason beyond themselves — a purpose to return to, a contribution yet to make.

The watch-out: Impact can become a new source of ego if we're not careful. "I want to change the world" can quietly mean "I want the world to know I changed it." True impact is often quiet, local, and unmeasured by any metric that trends on social media.

8
Truth
Pursuing understanding, honesty, and an accurate picture of reality

Truth is the rarest and most demanding pursuit. It means caring more about what is real than what is comfortable, more about understanding than about being right. It means being willing to discover that you were wrong about yourself, about others, about what matters — and updating accordingly.

The gift: Those who genuinely pursue truth tend to build lives of remarkable coherence. Their decisions are made with clearer information. Their relationships are deeper because they are more honest. Their sense of self is not built on performance or rank but on a clear-eyed relationship with reality.

The cost: Truth is not always kind. It requires dismantling comforting illusions about yourself and your circumstances. It is the goal that will lose you the most friends if you pursue it without also pursuing compassion — and gain you the most meaningful ones when you do.

The Tension Between Levels

It would be a mistake to read this hierarchy as a simple staircase where you graduate from one level and leave it behind. The reality is messier and more interesting. You will feel the pull of all eight at different times — sometimes in the same week. What matters is which goal is organizing your choices when it counts.

The tragedy of life is not that it ends so soon, but that we wait so long to begin it.
— W.M. Lewis

The lower levels are not eliminated as you ascend — they are integrated. Someone genuinely pursuing truth still wants to feel good (happiness), still cares about how they are perceived (cool, popularity), still wants to be respected (prestige), still needs financial stability (money). The difference is that those needs are no longer the organizing principle. They become context, not compass.

Externally Anchored Goals

  • Dependent on others' perception of you
  • Shift with social context and comparison
  • Vulnerable to circumstances outside your control
  • Can generate quick reward but fragile satisfaction
  • Often leave you asking "Is this it?"

Internally Anchored Goals

  • Defined by your own standards and values
  • Stable across social environments
  • Resilient in the face of failure or loss
  • Build compounding satisfaction over time
  • Generate the sense that your life is yours

Why So Many of Us Get Stuck at the Lower Levels

It's not weakness or lack of ambition that keeps people chasing popularity, prestige, or money long past the point of meaningful return. It's something more structural: the lower-level goals have more immediate feedback loops. When you post something and people respond, that's a dopamine hit in real time. When you earn a promotion, there's a ceremony. When you buy something beautiful, the feeling is immediate.

Excellence, impact, and truth are slower. They don't come with confetti. They require delayed gratification at a scale that our evolved brains were not naturally wired for. And perhaps most critically, they require tolerating uncertainty — not knowing yet whether your work is good enough, whether your contribution is landing, whether the truth you're pursuing is leading somewhere worth going.

Key Insight

The anxiety many people feel in their late twenties and thirties isn't a sign that something is wrong with them — it's often the first honest signal that the goals they've been optimizing for are no longer the goals that will sustain them. That discomfort is a compass, not a crisis.

How to Find Your Real Goal

The question isn't which goal sounds most noble when you say it out loud. It's which goal is actually driving your behavior when the stakes are real and no one is watching.

1

Trace Your Decisions Backward

Think of a major decision you made in the last year. What were you actually optimizing for? Be honest — not what you told people, but what you were really after.

2

Notice What Stings

What kinds of failure or rejection bother you most? We are most hurt by the things we care about most. Your sensitivities reveal your hierarchy.

3

Ask the Deathbed Question

Palliative care nurses consistently report that the dying regret time spent on image and status, not on love and meaning. Work backward from that.

4

Try Removing the Audience

If no one would ever know — no recognition, no social proof — would you still want to do the thing you're working toward? That answer is clarifying.

5

Notice Where You Invest Surplus Energy

What do you do with free time when no one is directing you? Not what you think you should do — what do you actually drift toward?

6

Sit With the Discomfort

If this reflection is uncomfortable, lean in. Discomfort at this level is a sign you've found something worth examining — not something to be avoided.

A Note of Caution

This framework can be weaponized into a new form of self-criticism — a way to feel bad about pursuing happiness, money, or popularity rather than truth and impact. That would be a misuse. The goal is awareness, not hierarchy-shaming. Knowing where you are is the prerequisite for choosing where you want to go.

The Real Question Is Integration

The most fulfilled people I've encountered are not those who have abandoned the lower levels of this hierarchy — they are those who have integrated them. They allow themselves to enjoy pleasure, care about how they're perceived, want financial security, and still organize their life around something deeper. The lower goals get their due, but they don't run the show.

Integration looks like this: you pursue excellence in your craft, which generates income (money), which earns you respect in your field (prestige), which brings you genuine friendships (popularity), which feels meaningful and beautiful (happiness). The hierarchy is all present — but anchored at the top, not the bottom.

He who has a why to live for can bear almost any how.
— Friedrich Nietzsche

Final Thoughts

We are all, at any given moment, in pursuit of something. The question is whether we have chosen it consciously — whether the goal organizing our days is one we would actually endorse on reflection, with full information, at the end of our lives.

You don't have to abandon what you're working toward. But it is worth pausing — even just for a few honest minutes — to ask: what am I actually pursuing here? Is this the goal I chose, or the one the world handed to me? And if I keep heading in this direction for another five years, will I be glad I went?

Those questions are harder to sit with than any exam, any performance review, any difficult conversation. But they are also the most important ones. Because you get one life. And how you aim it matters more than how fast you run.
