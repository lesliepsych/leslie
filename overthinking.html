Overthinking

"We suffer more in our imagination than in reality."

Leslie A. Esperida
March 31, 2026
10 min read

Ilang beses ka na bang nagdusa sa isang bagay na hindi pa naman nangyayari? Ilang gabi ka na bang gising, nag-iisip ng worst-case scenario — na sa totoo lang, hindi naman dumating? That's overthinking. And it's stealing more from your life than the actual problems ever will.

Nearly 2,000 years ago, the Stoic philosopher Seneca wrote words that remain hauntingly relevant today: "We suffer more often in imagination than in reality." He wasn't guessing. He was observing a fundamental truth about human psychology — that the mind, when left to run unchecked, creates monsters far more terrifying than anything life actually delivers.

Overthinking is not the same as careful planning or healthy reflection. It is the repetitive, unproductive loop of imagining the worst, replaying past mistakes, and mentally rehearsing disasters that usually never happen. It's the mental equivalent of running on a treadmill — exhausting, but you never actually go anywhere.

We suffer more often in imagination than in reality.
— Seneca, Letters from a Stoic

Imagination vs. Reality: Tingnan Natin

The best way to expose overthinking is to hold it up against what actually happens. Para makita mo kung gaano kalayo ang kinatatakutan mo versus ang totoong nangyayari — let's walk through five everyday scenarios that most of us know all too well.

💼

Job Interview

01
Imagination

"Baka bumagsak ako, baka mali sagot ko."

"Baka mapahiya ako sa harap nila."

"Baka i-judge nila yung outfit ko."

"Hindi ko alam ang isasagot ko."

Reality

Usually tinatanong lang: "Tell me about yourself."

Experience mo. Availability mo.

Friendly naman madalas ang interviewer.

After 10–20 minutes, tapos na. Hindi disaster.

Suffering  3 days kaba
VS
Reality  15 minutes lang
Mas mahaba yung suffering sa imagination.
💬

Text / Chat na 'Di Nagreply

02
Imagination

"Galit siguro sa akin."

"May mali akong sinabi."

"Ayaw na niya sa akin."

"Baka binlock na ako."

Reality

Busy lang. Tulog. Low battery.

Walang signal. Nakalimutan lang.

Nabasa pero naabutan ng meeting.

90% ng problema dito — gawa ng utak natin.

Suffering  5 hours kaba
VS
Reality  "Sorry busy lang!"
Lahat nung kinabahan ka, wala palang problema.
⚠️

Small Mistake at Work

03
Imagination

"Tatanggalin na ako."

"Galit na yung boss ko."

"Sira na reputation ko."

"Lahat napansin yung pagkakamali ko."

Reality

"Next time double check mo lang."

Tapos move on na lahat.

Walang pumapansin kasi may kanya-kanya silang trabaho.

Ikaw na lang nag-iisip buong gabi.

Suffering  2 days overthinking
VS
Reality  30-second correction
Move on na lahat — ikaw lang hindi.
🎤

Reporting / Public Speaking

04
Imagination

"Baka matawa sila sa akin."

"Baka ma-mental block ako."

"Baka mapahiya ako sa lahat."

"Baka manginig boses ko."

Reality

Nakikinig lang naman sila. May mga nag-no-notes pa.

Yung iba nagce-cellphone pa nga.

Pag natapos ka, clap lang tapos next na.

Ikaw lang nag-iisip na big deal.

Suffering  1 week dread
VS
Reality  5–10 minutes lang
7 days kaba, 10 minutes lang pala.
🔀

Making a Big Decision

05
Imagination

"Baka mag-fail ako."

"Baka mawalan ako ng pera."

"Baka mali ang decision ko."

"Baka pagsisihan ko 'to."

Reality

Kahit magkamali ka, nakakarecover naman ang tao.

Hindi katapusan ng mundo.

Most successful people failed multiple times.

Ang totoong risk? Yung hindi ka kumikilos dahil sa takot.

Suffering  Months of paralysis
VS
Reality  Adjustable naman pala
Nag-suffer ka ng months sa "baka" — hindi sa totoo.

The Pattern: Ano Ba Talaga ang Kinatatakutan Natin?

Kung titingnan mo nang maigi, may pattern ang lahat ng overthinking. Hindi naman talaga yung event ang kinakatakutan natin — it's something deeper. Almost all overthinking boils down to four core fears:

🚫

Rejection

"Baka tanggihan nila ako. Baka ayaw nila sa akin. Baka hindi ako tanggap."

😳

Embarrassment

"Baka mapahiya ako. Baka pagtawanan nila ako. Baka magmukhang tanga."

📉

Failure

"Baka hindi ko kaya. Baka bumagsak ako. Baka patunayan ko na kulang ako."

👥

Judgment

"Ano kaya sasabihin nila? Baka hindi maganda tingin sa akin. Baka ma-judge ako."

But Here's the Truth

People are too busy worrying about their own lives to spend time judging yours. Yung akala mong pinag-uusapan ka nila? Nag-iisip din sila ng sarili nilang "baka." Lahat tayo — trapped sa sarili nating imagination.

Why Does the Brain Do This?

It's easy to blame yourself for overthinking, but the truth is: your brain is doing its job — it's just doing it too well. The human brain evolved to detect threats. In prehistoric times, that constant scanning for danger kept us alive. But in the modern world, where our threats are social rejection and email typos instead of predators, that ancient alarm system misfires constantly.

This is what psychologists call the negativity bias — the brain's tendency to give more weight to negative possibilities than to positive or neutral ones. Your brain doesn't care about being happy. It cares about keeping you alive. And to keep you alive, it will always, always imagine the worst.

Key Insight

Research from Penn State University found that 91% of the things people worried about never came true. And of the 9% that did happen, the outcome was better than expected nearly half the time. Meaning: your brain is wrong about the future almost every single time it predicts disaster.

So the next time your brain tells you, "Baka ganito mangyari," remember — your brain is not a prophet. It's a survival machine stuck in a world it wasn't designed for. Its job is to scare you. Your job is to question whether the scare is real.

How to Stop the Loop: Practical Strategies

Overthinking is a habit — and like all habits, it can be interrupted, redirected, and eventually replaced with healthier patterns. Hindi ito overnight fix, but these strategies can start loosening the grip that overthinking has on your mind:

1

The 5-5-5 Rule

Ask yourself: "Will this matter in 5 days? 5 months? 5 years?" If the answer is no, it's probably not worth the mental energy you're spending right now. This creates instant perspective.

2

Set a "Worry Timer"

Give yourself 10 minutes to worry — fully, intensely, without guilt. When the timer ends, you stop. This teaches your brain that worrying has a boundary. Hindi siya allowed to hijack your whole day.

3

Write It Down

Overthinking thrives in the abstract. When you write your worries on paper, they become concrete — and usually much smaller than they seemed in your head. Nakikita mo na, "Ah, 'yan lang pala 'yun."

4

Challenge the Thought

When your brain says "Baka…," respond with: "Is there evidence for this? Or is this my imagination?" Most of the time, wala kang maipapakitang proof. Feeling lang 'yan, hindi fact.

5

Move Your Body

Overthinking lives in the mind. Physical movement — a walk, stretching, even cleaning — interrupts the loop by pulling your attention into your body. Hindi ka pwedeng mag-overthink habang humihingal ka.

6

Talk to Someone

Say it out loud. Share it with a friend, a family member, or a therapist. Kapag narinig mo na yung sarili mong worry out loud, madalas mararamdaman mo — "Ang silly pala pakinggan." And that's the point.

A Gentle Reminder

Overthinking is not a character flaw. It doesn't mean you're weak, broken, or crazy. It often means you care deeply — about outcomes, about people, about doing the right thing. The problem isn't that you think. The problem is that you think the same painful thought over and over again, mistaking repetition for preparation.

But preparation looks like planning and then acting. Overthinking looks like planning, re-planning, doubting the plan, imagining the plan failing, and then never acting at all.

Overthinking is not a sign of intelligence. It is a sign of not knowing how to manage your own mind. The goal is not to stop thinking — it's to stop letting your thoughts control you.
— a reminder for every overthinker

Ang dami nating oras na nasasayang sa pag-iisip ng mga bagay na hindi naman nangyayari. Ilang gabi na tayong gising dahil sa "baka." Ilang opportunities na ang pinalampas natin dahil sa takot na gawa-gawa lang ng utak natin.

So the next time you find yourself spiraling — the next time your brain starts playing that movie of everything that could go wrong — pause. Take a breath. And remind yourself:

Mas madalas, mas malala ang imagination mo kaysa sa reality.

The interview will be shorter than your anxiety. The reply will come. The mistake will be forgotten. The audience isn't judging you. And the decision you're afraid to make? It's almost never as permanent as your fear tells you it is.

Final Thoughts

Hindi mo kayang kontrolin lahat ng mangyayari sa buhay mo. But you can learn to control how much of your peace you surrender to things that haven't happened yet. The suffering that lives in your imagination is optional. The real moment — the one happening right now, while you're reading this — is almost always more manageable than your mind led you to believe.

You have survived 100% of your worst days. You have lived through every single thing your brain told you would destroy you. And you're still here. That track record is worth more than any "baka" your imagination can invent.

You don't have to believe everything you think.
— a truth worth remembering every single day
